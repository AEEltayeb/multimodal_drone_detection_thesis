These are the final, complete evaluation results across all three test domains.

Key Takeaways — Anti-UAV
IMPORTANT
RGB dominates on Anti-UAV. F1=0.99 is near-perfect. The RGB model almost never misses a drone (FN=639 out of ~80,000 positives) and fires very few false alarms (FP=921).
RGB is the star: F1=0.9902 (IoU) / 0.9925 (IoP). Nearly perfect.
IR is slightly weaker on recall: F1=0.9619. It misses 4,818 drone instances that RGB catches. This is likely due to thermal signature ambiguity (drone blends into warm backgrounds, or small drones lack contrast).
Classifier (meta-model) nearly matches RGB: F1=0.9916. It pools detections from both modalities (scoped GT), so it inherits IR's misses as additional FN and IR's extra FPs. It doesn't beat RGB alone because RGB is already so strong here.
Filter has near-zero impact: On Anti-UAV there are no confusers to filter. The filter removes 1–4 TPs and 1–4 FPs — statistically irrelevant. This proves the filter is safe — it doesn't randomly destroy detections when there are no confusers.
IoU vs IoP gap is small: Anti-UAV annotations are tight and well-aligned, so both matching strategies agree.

Explanation of Key Phenomena
Why Does "Drone" Appear as an FP Category?
This is the most confusing part of the results. A "DRONE" FP does not mean the model detected a non-drone and called it a drone. It means:
The frame does contain a drone, the model did detect something, but the detection box and the ground-truth box don't overlap enough (IoU < 0.5). The detection is scored as FP, and the unmatched GT is scored as FN.
Proof: Under IoP matching, RGB's "DRONE" FPs collapse from 6,905 → 72 (a 99% drop). The detections were correct the whole time — they were tight predictions inside Svanström's giant, padded GT boxes. IoU punishes this; IoP forgives it.
Why Is RGB Terrible on Svanström?
Two reasons stacking on top of each other:
Confusers: RGB correctly detects airplanes (3,315 FPs), birds (2,418), and helicopters (4,971) — it doesn't know these aren't drones. IR is nearly immune to these because its training data didn't include these confusers and their thermal signatures are different.
Loose GT boxes (IoU only): On top of the confuser problem, the tight RGB predictions inside oversized GT boxes get marked as FP. Switching to IoP fixes this, and RGB F1 jumps from 0.08 → 0.53.
The Classifier Is Remarkably Effective
Under IoP matching on Svanström:
Precision = 0.989 — only 208 FPs across 28,710 frames
It achieves this by routing decisions through the modality it trusts. Since IR is reliable on Svanström (no airplanes/helis in IR), the classifier learns to rely on IR and reject RGB-only detections
The FP breakdown confirms this: classifier produces zero airplane and helicopter FPs (RGB's biggest weakness), and only 19 bird FPs (IR's minor weakness).
The Filter's Value Is Modality-Specific
On raw RGB (the main use case for the filter):
TIP
The filter crushed 76% of all RGB false positives with only a moderate recall cost (69% → 65%). Helicopters and airplanes were almost entirely eliminated.
On the classifier (two orderings compared, scoped GT):
With scoped GT (classifier only penalized for misses in the modality it chose to trust), the classifier achieves F1=0.9937 — near-perfect. The correct ordering (classifier→filter) achieves the highest precision (0.9897, only 190 FPs) at a small recall cost (0.998 → 0.960). The classifier alone remains the best F1 when in-distribution confusers are the primary concern.
NOTE
GT Scoping: Classifier configs are scored only against the GT of the modality they chose to trust. This is the correct methodology — the classifier should not be penalized for unmatched GTs in a modality it explicitly rejected. On paired datasets (Anti-UAV) this has minimal effect; on unpaired datasets (Svanström) it eliminates ~4,000 spurious FNs.
NOTE
The old filter→classifier is architecturally wrong: it modifies the classifier's input features, feeding it a distribution it wasn't trained on. The correct classifier→filter preserves the classifier's trained routing decision and applies the filter as a post-hoc safety net.
On IR: The filter has minimal impact — IR had almost no confuser FPs to begin with (117 bird FPs → 73, that's it).
On Anti-UAV: The filter is dormant — no confusers exist in the dataset, so it changes nothing (±1-4 detections).
CAUTION
Caveat on IR's Svanström numbers: The IR model saw Svanström data during training. Its high F1 (0.96) partially reflects memorization, not pure generalization. Anti-UAV is the unbiased benchmark for IR.

3. YouTube OOD IR Filter Test (14 thermal videos)
Motivation
The classifier makes the confuser filter redundant on Svanström because IR never hallucinates on Svanström confusers — the classifier simply routes trust to IR and ignores RGB's false positives. But this relies on an assumption: the IR model won't fire on non-drone objects.
What happens when the IR model does hallucinate? In an out-of-distribution (OOD) deployment, thermal footage from different cameras, distances, and environments may cause the IR YOLO model to detect airplanes, helicopters, and birds. In this scenario, the classifier can no longer suppress FPs by trusting IR, and the confuser filter becomes the last line of defence.
To test this, we evaluated 14 YouTube thermal/IR videos — real-world OOD footage that the IR model and filter have never seen during training.
Test Design
Two configs: ir_only (raw IR YOLO) vs ir_filter (IR YOLO + patch verifier at P(confuser) ≥ 0.70 veto threshold)
Every frame is assumed to contain the labeled object (these are tracking/surveillance videos)
Confuser videos (airplane/bird/helicopter): any detection = False Positive → filter should suppress
Drone videos: any detection = True Positive → filter should preserve

NOTE

Key Takeaways — YouTube OOD
IMPORTANT
The confuser filter is a genuine safety net for OOD IR deployment. On the clean drone video, it preserves 80.4% of 89.0% detection rate (only 9.6% recall loss), while suppressing 81% of helicopter and 55% of total confuser hallucinations.
The IR model does hallucinate on OOD confusers: The helicopter video (yt_EdOX8tJZDzw) triggers IR detections on 40.5% of frames — a catastrophic false positive rate if deployed without the filter.
The filter dramatically suppresses helicopter FPs: 40.5% → 7.6% (81.3% reduction). This is the filter's strongest result, because helicopters produce the most convincing drone-like thermal signatures.
Drone recall impact is modest on clean video: The clean drone video (yt_zFu7hAi5mIc) drops from 89.0% → 80.4% — a 9.6% recall loss. The rejected detections were edge-case frames where the drone's thermal signature resembled a helicopter (the filter's most common confusion on drones).
LABELS-quality drone videos show minimal filter impact: yt_Y0epqCI7muk lost only 0.7%, yt_oA8Bfc_bjFk lost 4.5%. The filter is generally conservative on drone patches.
yt_nqk0NsTBlFI inflated suppression (38.9%): This video has a software UI overlay, and the filter is correctly rejecting detections on UI elements (labeled bird:0.92) rather than on actual drone patches.

Thesis-Ready Conclusions
Single-modality performance is domain-dependent: RGB excels on Anti-UAV (F1=0.99) but collapses on Svanström (F1=0.08 IoU / 0.53 IoP) due to confusers. IR is consistent across both (~0.95-0.96). This proves the need for multi-modal fusion.
The meta-classifier is the best general-purpose strategy: On Anti-UAV it achieves F1=0.9916 (near RGB). On Svanström it achieves F1=0.9937 (IoP) — massively outperforming rgb_only while maintaining IR's confuser immunity. It does this with only 208 total FPs across 28,710 frames.
The confuser filter is a targeted RGB remedy and IR safety net:
On Svanström (in-distribution): It cuts RGB FPs by 76% with no measurable cost on Anti-UAV. On the classifier, it's redundant because the classifier already routes around RGB confusers.
On YouTube OOD (out-of-distribution): It proves its value as a safety net for IR deployment. When the IR model hallucinates on OOD thermal confusers (helicopters: 40.5% FP rate), the filter suppresses 81% of helicopter FPs while preserving 90% of drone recall on clean footage.
The classifier + filter architecture is complementary, not redundant: The classifier handles the expected failure mode (RGB confusers in known environments). The filter handles the unexpected failure mode (IR hallucinations on OOD thermal sources). Together they provide defence-in-depth:
Classifier: modality routing (trust IR when RGB is unreliable)
Filter: per-detection verification (catch the cases where the trusted modality itself fires incorrectly)
IoP is the honest metric for Svanström: The IoU-vs-IoP gap on DRONE-category FPs (6,905 → 72 for RGB) proves that annotation quality, not model quality, drives the IoU scores on this dataset.
CAUTION
Caveat on IR's Svanström numbers: The IR model saw Svanström data during training. Its high F1 (0.96) partially reflects memorization, not pure generalization. Anti-UAV is the unbiased benchmark for IR.
