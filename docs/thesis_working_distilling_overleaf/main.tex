\chapter*{Abstract}

Protecting a site against consumer drones requires high recall and a low false-alarm rate simultaneously. The single-stage detectors trained in this work are already strong on both counts on ordinary footage: the production RGB detector raises 41 false positives across 4{,}000 Anti-UAV frames and hallucinates on $2.8\%$ of its composite test corpus, and on much non-confuser footage it raises no false alarm at all. The false-alarm problem is specific to scenes containing aerial \emph{confusers} (birds, airplanes, helicopters): there the same detector fires on $30.4\%$ of an out-of-distribution confuser corpus and on up to $94\%$ of bird-only Svanstr\"om~\cite{svanstrom2021real} frames, and suppressing confusers by retraining the detector collapses small-drone recall ($0.961 \to 0.306$). This thesis builds, and evaluates under a single declared protocol, a drone-detection \emph{system} in which every stage exists for a stated purpose: an RGB detector for daytime detection and a thermal detector for night and low-visibility conditions, a per-frame trust classifier that routes between them, a per-detection MLP \emph{confuser filter} that reads the detector's own ROI features to suppress the known false-positive classes, and a temporal aggregator that tracks the target and rejects transient hallucinations, behind an operator GUI.
% [source: thesis_eval/results/tier1_results.json (antiuav/rgb_dataset_test/rgb_confuser); ledger=retrainedv2-recall-collapse]

Because the bare detectors are already good, the pipeline has to prove its worth precisely where they fail. Under one evaluation standard with bootstrap confidence intervals, the full pipeline lifts Svanstr\"om paired drone F1 from $0.742$ to $0.944$ while \emph{raising} recall ($0.948 \to 0.991$); on the out-of-distribution confuser corpus it cuts frame fire from $30.4\%$ to $1.1\%$ (a router variant that adds a reject class reaches $0.15\%$ composed, at a recall cost on clean and single-modality surfaces we judged not worth paying); and on the saturated Anti-UAV control it does no harm ($0.973 \to 0.984$). An ablation isolates every stage: the filter suppresses the false positives and the reject class is a complementary net we trade for recall, composition order is a recall/precision dial, and the stages cost $0.095$~ms and $\sim$2~ms respectively ($37$--$404\times$ cheaper than the hand-engineered classifier and patch-CNN predecessors they replaced), so everything runs on every frame.
% [source: thesis_eval/results/tier1_results.json (svanstrom/antiuav B, rgb_confuser C); ledger=robust6-speed-feature-efficiency,v5-ship-per-frame]

The components are products of two methodological contributions. A \emph{statistics-before-training} instrument (the Model MRI) measures what a detector's feature space can separate before anything is trained: it found drone/confuser structure linearly separable inside both detectors' own feature corpora (LDA separability $0.952$ RGB, $0.981$ IR), selected the trust classifier's eight leakage-free features, aligned grayscale into thermal feature space (transfer AUROC $0.500 \to 0.919$), and trains the production confuser filters automatically from its own measurements. A \emph{human-in-the-loop data engine} (a purpose-built label reviewer and a disciplined dataset--model co-evolution loop) was a necessity rather than a choice: clean thermal drone data is far scarcer than RGB, so the IR detector and its corpus had to be co-developed. The loop carried the IR detector from F1 $0.503$ to $0.967$, every revision scored on the same fixed held-out test split, including the one revision that bypassed review and regressed, reported as the loop's clearest negative result. Two findings emerged unplanned. The thermal-trained detector, fed grayscale-converted RGB, performs within $2.7$~pp F1 of the dedicated RGB detector at matched configuration; and grayscale-harvested confusers, carried across the measured modality alignment, are what made the thermal confuser filter trainable at all.
