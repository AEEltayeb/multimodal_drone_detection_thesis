\chapter*{Abstract}
% ~ alt + 126 
The increasing adoption of Artificial Intelligence (AI) has highlighted the need for transparency, accountability, and traceability in AI systems. Inspired by the concept of \textbf{Software Bill of Materials (\sbom)} in traditional software, this work introduces \textbf{ALOHA}, a tool for generating \textbf{AI Bill of Materials (\aibom)} from models hosted on \textbf{Hugging Face}, using the \textbf{CycloneDX standard} in JSON format.\\ 
ALOHA automates metadata extraction from model cards, generating structured inventories that document key components such as architecture, datasets, performance metrics, and environmental considerations. This systematic approach ensures standardized documentation of AI models, enhancing transparency, trust, and compliance with emerging regulations.\\
To assess the accuracy of the \aiboms generated by ALOHA, an empirical evaluation was conducted, combining both quantitative and qualitative analyses. 
The \textbf{goal} of this study is to assess whether the \aiboms generated by ALOHA include all the expected data fields, and if any fields are missing, to understand the reasons behind their absence. \\The study analyzed a statistically significant random sample of 312 AI models, selected from 1,643 publicly available models on Hugging Face based on predefined inclusion criteria (at least 100 downloads and 100 likes) to ensure relevance and reduce bias.\\
The \textbf{results} show that the most frequently present fields are those that can be manually added or automatically inferred by Hugging Face when a model is uploaded. However, many fields in the generated \aiboms remain empty due to the absence of information in the model cards. 
These findings underscore the need for \textbf{improved} documentation practices of AI systems.\\

\chapter{Introduction}

\textbf{Software Bill of Materials} (\sboms) have become a crucial component of software supply chain security, providing a comprehensive inventory of the components and dependencies used within software projects. They enhance vulnerability management, licensing compliance, and overall transparency by enabling organizations to track and assess the security of third-party and open-source software components. Various standards, such as \textbf{SPDX} and \textbf{CycloneDX}, have been developed to standardize \sboms and make them machine-readable for automated analysis.\\
The increasing adoption of artificial intelligence (AI) systems has brought significant advancements and raised concerns regarding transparency, accountability, and security. As AI models become increasingly complex and widely used, ensuring traceability in their development and deployment is essential. While \sboms address transparency challenges in traditional software, they fall short when applied to AI systems, which rely not only on software dependencies but also on datasets, model architectures, training procedures, and performance metrics. \textbf{AI Bill of Materials (\aiboms)} extend the principles of \sbom to AI, ensuring that critical information about AI models is \textbf{structured} and \textbf{accessible}. By documenting key elements such as datasets, model architecture, and evaluation metrics, \aiboms improve reproducibility, risk assessment, and compliance in AI development and deployment. That is, inspired by the concept of \sbom in traditional software, \aiboms have been proposed as \textbf{structured inventories} that document AI models, datasets, algorithms, and dependencies, enhancing \textbf{transparency} and \textbf{accountability} in AI systems.

The importance of \aiboms is further underscored by recent regulatory developments. On May 12, 2021, President Joe Biden issued \textbf{Executive Order (EO) 14028}, titled `Improving the Nation’s Cybersecurity', which mandates that software vendors working with the U.S. federal government must provide an SBOM. Given the rapid growth of AI and its integration into critical systems, it is likely that similar requirements will soon extend to AIBoMs, ensuring transparency and accountability in AI systems.\\
Open-source platforms like Hugging Face play a pivotal role in the AI ecosystem. They provide access to a vast collection of datasets and pre-trained models, significantly reducing the computational resources required for training and promoting environmental sustainability. However, as the adoption of AI models grows, so does the need for standardized documentation to ensure compliance with emerging regulations and best practices.

This thesis presents \textbf{ALOHA}, a tool designed to automate the generation of \aiboms by extracting relevant information from model cards available on Hugging Face. Using the \textbf{CycloneDX} standard in JSON format, ALOHA enables structured documentation of AI components, facilitating better compliance, security, and reproducibility in AI systems. By standardizing the documentation of AI models, ALOHA not only supports compliance with emerging regulations but also fosters transparency and trust in AI systems.\\
The study also includes an empirical evaluation to assess the accuracy of the AIBOMs generated by ALOHA, verifying whether they include all the expected data fields and, if any are missing, identifying the reasons behind their absence. The results show that the most frequently present fields are those that, if not explicitly provided by the model card authors, are automatically inferred and added by Hugging Face. Conversely, the missing fields in the generated \aibom are primarily due to the absence of corresponding information in the model cards.
By addressing the challenges of AI transparency and standardization, this research contributes to the broader effort of responsible AI deployment.

The thesis is structured as follows. 
Chapter II provides background information and review related work, introducing an overview of \aibom, \sbom, and the Hugging Face platform. It establishes the foundation for understanding the context and relevance of the proposed tool. 
Chapter III presents ALOHA, the tool developed to generate \aiboms from model cards available on Hugging Face. This chapter explains the rationale behind choosing CycloneDX as the \aibom standard and describes how information is collected. It also outlines the proposed \aibom model, detailing its structure and the information it contains. Finally, it discusses the mapping between Hugging Face fields and CycloneDX, explaining how information extracted from Hugging Face is organized within the CycloneDX schema. 
Chapter IV outlines the design of the empirical study, describing the methodology and approach adopted for data collection and analysis. It then analyzes the results, discussing their significance and potential threats to validity. Chapter V concludes the thesis, summarizing the key findings, contributions, and potential future work.

\section{\sbom}
In recent years, the software industry has increasingly focused on addressing the challenges of software supply chain security, dependency management, and transparency. One of the key solutions to these challenges is the \textbf{\sbom}, which provides a comprehensive inventory of components, dependencies, and libraries used within a software project. \sboms play a critical role in understanding the potential risks associated with third-party software and ensuring compliance with licensing and security requirements. With the growing adoption of open-source software, \sboms have become even more essential to track and manage software components and their associated vulnerabilities.
Approaches like \sboms have become essential due to the nature of modern software development. Contemporary software often integrates both proprietary and open-source components, leading to intricate supply chains where the overall security depends on the most vulnerable component~\cite{10336262}.
The adoption of \sbom generation tools by open-source projects on GitHub began in 2014. However, a significantly greater interest has been observed since 2021, coinciding with major events such as President Biden's executive order to enhance the security of the software supply chain and the publication of the SPDX standard as ISO/IEC 5962:2021~\cite{10336262}.\\
Despite the growing importance of using \sboms in both industrial and open-source contexts, their adoption is still limited, as demonstrated by recent studies.
This is due to several factors~\cite{BOMs_Away!}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Complexity of \sbom specifications.
    \item Determining data fields to include in \sboms.
    \item Incompatibility between \sbom standards.
    \item Keeping \sboms up to date.
    \item Insufficient \sbom tooling.
    \item Inaccurate and incomplete \sboms
\end{itemize}
The \textbf{NTIA} defines an ideal \sbom as a document that captures fundamental details about software components~\cite{NTIA}, including:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Supplier Name
    \item Component Name
    \item Version of the Component
    \item Other Unique Identifiers 
    \item Dependency Relationship
    \item Author of \sbom Data
    \item Timestamp
\end{itemize}
Trevor Stalnaker et al.~\cite{BOMs_Away!} conducted a series of interviews with participants who were familiar with \sboms, members of critical open-source projects, AI/ML practitioners, cyber-physical systems experts, and legal professionals. These participants proposed the use of Bills of Materials (BOMs) for various technological contexts, including:
external services and APIs, defined as SaaSBOMs~\cite{cyclonedx_website}~\cite{CloudSecurityAlliance2022};
hardware (HBOMs)~\cite{CycloneDX2022HBOM} and firmware (FBOMs)~\cite{Nelson2021}~\cite{Costin2022}~\cite{Beer2022};
operational environments (e.g., configurations), referred to as OBOMs~\cite{CycloneDX2022OBOM};
datasets, through DataBOMs~\cite{Barclay2019};
and AI models, represented as \aiboms~\cite{Chan2017}~\cite{Xia2023} [45, 129].\\
These proposals reflect the \textbf{evolution} of the \sbom concept into more specific contexts and highlight the growing awareness of the need for transparency, security, and traceability across various technological domains.

\subsection{\sbom Standards: CycloneDX and SPDX}
Different \textbf{standards} have been developed to make \sboms machine-readable, enabling their integration with tools like vulnerability or license scanners. These standards provide a uniform schema to describe software composition. Among the most widely adopted standards are \textbf{SPDX}~\cite{spdx_website} and \textbf{CycloneDX}~\cite{cyclonedx_website}, as highlighted by Xia~\etal~\cite{10.1109/ICSE48619.2023.00219}.
The SPDX standard, originating from the Linux Foundation, was initially designed to address issues of software license compliance~\cite{10.1145/2950290.2983939},~\cite{10.1016/j.jss.2016.06.064}. Over time, its scope was expanded to include use cases related to software security. In 2021, it achieved recognition as an ISO/IEC standard~\cite{iso_81870}. Similarly, CycloneDX, developed by OWASP, was created to address various needs, ranging from license compliance to security. Both SPDX and CycloneDX are open standards, and their adoption is facilitated by the availability of tools capable of generating \sboms that are compliant with these specifications.
\section{\aibom}
\aibom is a structured inventory that details the components, models, datasets, algorithms, and dependencies used in the development, training, and deployment of an AI system. Inspired by the concept of \sbom in traditional software, \aibom aims to enhance \textbf{transparency}, \textbf{traceability}, and \textbf{security} in AI systems. It serves as a critical tool for risk management, compliance, and accountability in the development and deployment of AI.\\
With the rapid evolution of AI systems, traditional approaches to software supply chain transparency, such as \sbom, are no longer sufficient. While \sboms effectively track the components within software applications, they fall short in addressing the complexity and unique characteristics of AI systems. These systems often integrate diverse elements, including machine learning models, datasets, and specialized algorithms, which need to be accounted for to ensure transparency, traceability, and security.\\
The transition from \sbom to \aibom is thus a natural progression.
Unlike \sboms, which focus on source code and executable components, \aiboms are centered on \textbf{machine learning models} and their associated metadata, such as weights, variables, and configurations.
Besides the fundamental differences in their generation, it is important to highlight that while \sboms can help detect security vulnerabilities, \aiboms do not directly provide security-related information. Instead, \aiboms focus on the transparency of the model’s structural characteristics and configurations~\cite{Driving_AI}.\\
As AI models increasingly become open-source, their accessibility to a wide audience fosters democratization but also raises concerns about potential misuse by malicious actors. 
This is particularly significant as AI is being adopted in critical domains such as government and financial institutions, often without adequate safeguards for data protection and system resilience. These developments underline the urgent need for standardized tools like \aiboms to ensure transparency, mitigate risks, and promote the responsible adoption of AI technologies~\cite{Driving_AI}.\\ 
 In the text~\cite{BOMs_Away!}, the issue is raised of whether to separate the dataset information from the model information in \aiboms, which are also referred to by another notation, \textbf{MLBOM}.
CycloneDX introduced the fields for including information about AI systems in version 1.4 of their standard, released in 2022.  This update added the capability to describe AI and machine learning components in \sbom files, enabling better tracking and management of AI-related components. CycloneDX refers to these as MLBOMs~\cite{CycloneDXMLBOM}.

\section{Hugging Face HUB}
Hugging Face Hub~\cite{huggingface2025} is an open platform designed for hosting and sharing resources in the field of machine learning, fostering collaboration among researchers, developers, and practitioners. It features four key components:  
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Repositories}: Centralized storage for models, datasets, and code, enabling version control, seamless sharing, and reuse of resources. 
    \item \textbf{Models}: Pre-trained machine learning models across various tasks, such as natural language processing, computer vision, and audio processing, which users can directly integrate or fine-tune for specific applications. 
    \item \textbf{Datasets}: Curated and user-contributed collections of labeled or unlabeled data designed to support training, evaluation, and benchmarking of machine learning models.
    \item \textbf{Spaces}: Interactive web applications built using frameworks like Gradio or Streamlit, providing a user-friendly interface to demonstrate, test, and deploy machine learning models or tools.
\end{itemize}
This hub serves as a cornerstone of the pre-trained model (PTM) ecosystem, facilitating transparency, reuse, and innovation in AI development.

Each model on Hugging Face is accompanied by a \textbf{model card}, and each dataset is associated with a \textbf{dataset card}. Model cards are files that provide useful information about the model, helping users to better understand the model and its intended use. 
As detailed in Mitchell~\etal~\cite{Mitchell_2019}, a model card should include information on:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Model description}: technical details, architecture, version, and intended uses.
    %\item Its intended uses and potential limitations, including biases and ethical considerations as detailed in Mitchell~\etal~\cite{Mitchell_2019}
    \item \textbf{Training and evaluation data}: data sources, balance, and potential biases in the datasets.
    \item \textbf{Evaluation metrics}: performance across different demographic groups and usage contexts, highlighting any disparities.
    \item \textbf{Limitations and risks}: A discussion of the model's weaknesses, edge cases, or scenarios where it may underperform.
    \item \textbf{Ethical considerations}: potential risks of discrimination, data transparency, and measures to mitigate negative impacts.
\end{itemize}
Similarly, dataset cards provide detailed information about datasets, ensuring that users can understand the data's origin, collection methods, and any potential limitations related to using the dataset.\\
Documenting a model properly is crucial, as highlighted by Jones~\etal~\cite{jones_What_do_we}, who emphasizes that the most reused models are those with better documentation.

 Pepe~\etal~\cite{How_do_Hugging} analyzed approximately 159,000 pre-trained models hosted on the Hugging Face hub to evaluate the level of transparency regarding the training datasets, prediction biases, and declared licenses. Their findings reveal significant gaps in transparency, with only 14\%
 of the models explicitly tagging the datasets used, 18\%
 acknowledging potential biases, and 31\%
 declaring a license, highlighting the need for improved documentation and licensing compliance.

\section{Design Goals and Standard Selection}
\aibom is a structured inventory that details the components, models, datasets, algorithms, and dependencies used in the development, training, and deployment of an AI system.
The \textbf{goal} of this study is to develop a tool for the automatic generation of an \aibom from a repository hosted on Hugging Face. %repository or models?
To achieve this, existing standard formats for \aibom representation were analyzed, assessing their ability to effectively capture the complexity of AI systems. Among the available options, SPDX and CycloneDX, two widely adopted \sbom standards, were considered. After a thorough comparison, CycloneDX was selected as the preferred standard, as it is the most widely used among open-source projects~\cite{10336262} and offers a flexible, extensible structure suited for documenting AI models, datasets, and dependencies.
Originally released in 2018, CycloneDX introduced MLBOM in June 2023 with version 1.5~\cite{cyclonedx_history}, adding support for AI systems by enabling structured documentation of models, datasets, and dependencies.\\
CycloneDX standardizes model cards in a structured format, enabling the inventory of models and datasets to be used \textbf{independently} or in \textbf{combination} with other BOMs, such as HBOMs, \sboms, and SaaSBOMs. This flexibility ensures that AI-related components can be documented separately or as part of a broader software, hardware, and service inventory~\cite{cyclonedx_authoritative_guide}.


ALOHA is designed to generate an \aibom \textbf{independently}, leveraging the information provided in the model cards available on Hugging Face. These model cards contain essential metadata about the AI models, including their intended uses, potential limitations, training data, and evaluation results. By extracting this information, the tool generates a comprehensive \aibom that documents the AI model, its associated datasets, and dependencies.\\
The generated \aibom can be easily integrated with other types of BOMs, such as \sboms, HBOMs, and SaaSBOMs. This makes it possible to combine AI-specific details with broader software, hardware, and service inventories, ensuring a unified, transparent view of the entire system. 
\section{Analysis of Hugging Face Documentation}
\textbf{Model cards} are structured documents that provide essential information about machine learning models, including their intended use, limitations, ethical considerations, and performance metrics. Introduced by Mitchell~\etal~\cite{Model_Cards_for}, model cards aim to improve transparency and accountability in AI by offering standardized documentation that helps users understand a model’s capabilities and potential biases.
While model cards provide a framework for documenting machine learning models, a similar approach has been proposed for datasets through Datasheets for Datasets, introduced by Gebru~\etal~\cite{gebru2021datasheetsdatasets}. Datasheets for Datasets are structured documents designed to accompany datasets, offering critical information about their composition, collection process, intended use, and potential biases. Just as Model Cards aim to improve transparency in model deployment, Datasheets for Datasets seek to address the growing need for accountability in dataset creation and usage.
Datasheets for Datasets typically include details such as the motivation for dataset creation, data collection methodology, preprocessing steps, distribution, and ethical considerations. By providing this information, datasheets enable users to better understand the context and limitations of a dataset, which is crucial for ensuring responsible AI development. For instance, knowing the demographic composition of a dataset can help practitioners identify potential biases that may affect model performance or fairness.\\
The \textbf{combination} of model cards and datasheets for datasets creates a comprehensive documentation ecosystem for AI systems.\\
Hugging Face uses \textbf{model cards} to document machine learning models and \textbf{dataset cards} to document datasets. \\
The model cards are saved in the README.md file of each model repository and have two key parts, with overlapping information:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Metadata}
    \item \textbf{Text descriptions}
\end{itemize}
A model repository will render its README.md as a model card. The model card is a Markdown file, with a YAML section at the top that contains metadata about the model.
The metadata in the model card enhances model discovery and usability. For example, it enables:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Filtering models at Hugging Face Models.
    \item Displaying the model’s license.
    \item Linking datasets used for training by adding a message such as `Datasets used to train:' on the model’s page, if the datasets are available on the Hub.
\end{itemize}
There are multiple ways to add metadata to a model card, including:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Using the metadata user interface (UI).%, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:UI_metadata}.

  \end{tcolorbox}
  \caption{Metadata UI}
  \label{fig:UI_metadata}
\end{figure}
    \item Directly editing the YAML section of the README.md file.\begin{figure}[h!]

    \item Using the huggingface\_hub Python library~\cite{huggingfacehub}.
    \item Using the Model Card Creator Tool, to ease card creation without needing to program~\cite{hf_model_card_creator_tool}.
\end{itemize}



Additionally, many libraries with Hub integration will automatically add metadata to the model card when a model is uploaded.\\
Using the UI is the easiest way to add metadata to a Model Card, but it doesn’t support all metadata fields. If metadata that isn’t supported by the UI is required, the YAML section of the README.md file can be edited directly.
Hugging Face provides a \textbf{template for model metadata}~\cite{hf_model_card_metadata}.

Although metadata offer a structured summary, they may not capture all relevant details. In such cases, textual descriptions provide a more comprehensive narrative about the model's development, intended applications, and potential biases.
Hugging Face provides a \textbf{model card Template}~\cite{hf_model_card_template} to promote transparency and responsible AI development.
The creation of this template was informed by a landscape analysis of model documentation and a focus on making the model card both user-friendly and flexible for diverse audiences~\cite{ huggingface_model_card_guidebook}.
While using this template is not mandatory, it serves as a valuable guideline for documenting machine learning models.

The template suggests contributions from three key roles:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Developers}: responsible for technical details, training procedures, and evaluation results.
    \item \textbf{Sociotechnical experts}: address biases, risks, and ethical considerations.
    \item \textbf{Project organizers}: oversee documentation, usage guidelines, and broader model context.
\end{itemize}
Each section of the template includes instructions to facilitate completion, with placeholders for relevant details. While optional, adopting this structure enhances clarity and accessibility for model users.

Dataset cards serve the same purpose as model cards but focus on documenting datasets instead of models. Like model cards, dataset cards include a YAML metadata section and a narrative part that details the dataset’s origin, intended use, and potential biases~\cite{huggingface_datasets}.
Hugging Face also provides a \textbf{dataset card template}~\cite{hf_DatasetCardTemplate},  and a \textbf{template for dataset metadata}~\cite{hf_DatasetCard_metadata}.  While optional, these templates help standardize dataset descriptions, making them more accessible and informative for users.

\section{\aibom Structure}

ALOHA was developed using Python. It extracts relevant information from Hugging Face model cards and structures the data according to the CycloneDX standard, ensuring compatibility with established software component analysis frameworks.
A key aspect of this process is the mapping of fields from Hugging Face model cards to the corresponding elements in the CycloneDX schema.  This systematic mapping enables accurate and structured documentation of AI models, enhancing traceability, security, and compliance.
In the following sections, a detailed explanation of the mapping process is provided, outlining how various pieces of information are translated into CycloneDX-compliant elements.

CycloneDX adopts an \textbf{object-based} model and is defined in JSON Schema, XML Schema, and Protocol Buffers. For this implementation, the JSON schema was chosen. 
The root element of a CycloneDX BOM is BOM, which serves as the container for all other objects. Figure~\ref{fig:object_types} presents the object types defined in CycloneDX and provides a brief description of each object.

CycloneDX does not require the inclusion of all objects and their respective properties. Instead, it provides a flexible structure that allows incorporating only the relevant information based on the available data. In this implementation, only the information extracted from Hugging Face that was deemed useful for generating the \aibom is included.

Among the various objects in CycloneDX, Metadata and Components are the two main ones:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Metadata} provides additional information about the BOM itself, including details such as the timestamp, lifecycle, authors of the BOM, and information about the primary component it describes.
    \item \textbf{Components} is a list of software and hardware elements related to the primary component. Each entry in this list represents an individual component with its attributes.
\end{itemize}

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Immagini/BOM.png} 
    \caption{Description of the object types in CycloneDX.}
    \label{fig:object_types}
\end{figure}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Storing the model’s information within the \textbf{component field of metadata} as illustrated in the Figure \ref{fig:object_types_metadata}.

        \end{figure}
    \item Storing the model’s information within \textbf{the component field of components} as illustrated in the Figure \ref{fig:object_types_components}.
            \begin{figure}[h!]

            \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Immagini/COMPONENTS_.png}

            \label{fig:object_types_components}
        \end{figure}
\end{enumerate}



Choosing the \textbf{first solution} results in an \aibom specific to the machine learning model being described.  
Conversely, the \textbf{second solution} creates an \aibom that can be easily integrated into a broader BOM describing an application or software that utilizes the given machine learning model.


The first solution, which involves storing the model’s information within the component field of metadata, was chosen because it allows for a concise and self-contained representation of the AI model within the \aibom. 
Moreover, this structure does not prevent future integration within a broader BOM. If needed, the \aibom can be incorporated into another BOM by simply moving the contents of the component field from metadata to components, allowing for seamless compatibility with a more extensive software or application description.

Another important consideration concerns how to include dataset information. The dataset details can either be embedded within the component describing the machine learning model (Figure~\ref{fig:dataset_1}) or stored as a separate component (Figure~\ref{fig:dataset_2}). The choice depends on whether the dataset is considered an integral part of the model or an independent entity that may be referenced separately within the BOM structure.
In this work, the dataset is stored as a separate component, as this approach enhances modularity, reusability, and clarity within the \aibom. Since datasets can be used by multiple machine learning models, treating them as independent components avoids redundancy and allows for better dependency management.

\section{Methodology for Collecting Information}
As mentioned earlier, model cards are saved in the README.md file of each model repository on Hugging Face and consist of two key parts with overlapping information: \textbf{metadata} and \textbf{text description}.
\subsection{Metadata}
For the metadata, the Hugging Face Endpoints API~\cite{huggingface_api} is leveraged. Specifically, a query is performed to retrieve a JSON entry for a specific model hosted on Hugging Face, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:HF_API}. Each JSON entry contains various details about the model, including the last modification date, model tags, number of downloads, license, and more.

\begin{figure}[h!]
  \centering
  \begin{tcolorbox}[colback=white, colframe=black, boxrule=1.5pt, arc=2mm, width=0.9\textwidth]
  \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{Immagini/HF_API_l.png}
  \end{tcolorbox}
  \caption{Hugging Face Endpoints API}
  \label{fig:HF_API}
\end{figure}



\subsection{Text description}
For the text description, the \texttt{README.md} file is directly retrieved from each model repository on Hugging Face. To achieve this, an HTTP request is performed to the raw content of the \texttt{README.md} file using its corresponding URL:

\begin{center}
\texttt{https://huggingface.co/\{model\_id\}/raw/main/README.md}
\end{center}

Where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\texttt{/raw}} indicates that a \textit{raw} (unprocessed) version of the file is being requested.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{/main/}} refers to the \textit{main branch} of the repository.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{README.md}} is the \textit{specific file name} to be retrieved.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[h!]
  \centering
  \begin{tcolorbox}[colback=white, colframe=black, boxrule=1.5pt, arc=2mm, width=0.9\textwidth]
  \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{Immagini/HF_readme_l.png}
  \end{tcolorbox}
  \caption{Hugging Face Endpoints API}
  \label{fig:HF_readme}
\end{figure}

Once the content of the \texttt{README.md} file has been retrieved, the fact that model cards are written in Markdown format is leveraged. This allows for identifying section titles by detecting lines that start with the \texttt{\#} symbol. The corresponding content is extracted, and the process terminates when one of the following conditions is met:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The beginning of a new section, indicated by another \texttt{\#} symbol.
    \item The end of the model card.
\end{itemize}
By using this approach, the textual content of the model card can be extracted.

The BOM element has the following properties:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{bomFormat}: specifies the format of the BOM. This value must be "CycloneDX".
    \item \textit{specVersion}: the version of the CycloneDX specification the BOM conforms to (The latest version, which is 1.6, is used.).

    \item \textit{version}: the version of the BOM, the default value is 1, and it is incremented when the BOM is modified.
\end{itemize}
The properties \textit{serialNumber} and \textit{version} form the \textbf{identity} of a BOM.

\section{Limitations}
This section outlines the key limitations of ALHOA. One of the most significant challenges is that the quality and completeness of the generated \aiboms are inherently dependent on the information provided by model authors. Since ALHOA extracts data directly from model cards, any inconsistencies, omissions, or inaccuracies in the original documentation will be reflected in the resulting \aiboms.
 For example, if authors do not provide detailed descriptions of datasets, architectures, or performance metrics, the \aibom will lack critical information.
This reliance on user-generated content introduces variability in the quality of the \aiboms, as not all authors follow the same documentation standards or provide the same level of detail. Consequently, the tool's output is only as reliable as the input it receives, which may limit its effectiveness in generating comprehensive and accurate \aiboms.

The tool was developed and tested specifically for models hosted on Hugging Face, which means its applicability to other platforms or ecosystems may be limited. Other platforms may have different standards or templates for model documentation, which could make it difficult to directly apply the tool without significant modifications.\\

\chapter{Empirical evaluation}
In this chapter, an empirical evaluation, both quantitative and qualitative, of ALOHA in the generation of \aiboms from Hugging Face AI models is presented.

\section{Study Design}
In this section, the design of the empirical evaluation is outlined, detailing the goal and research question, the study context and planning, and the approach to data analysis.

\subsection{Goal and Research Question}
The primary goal of this evaluation is to assess the accuracy of \aiboms generated by ALOHA. This evaluation is approached from the perspective of practitioners working with AI models and researchers focused on the AI/software supply chain. 
The context of the study involves AI models publicly hosted on Hugging Face.
Based on this goal, the following Research Question (RQ) was formulated:

\textbf{RQ}:\textit{ How accurate is ALOHA in generating \aiboms?}

This question aims to evaluate whether the \aiboms generated by ALOHA include all the expected data fields, and if any fields are missing, to understand why. The rationale behind this is to determine whether ALOHA can successfully retrieve all necessary information from Hugging Face AI models to generate accurate \aiboms.

\subsection{Study Context and Planning}
The context of the study includes AI models publicly available on Hugging Face.
To search for these models, the Hugging Face API Endpoints~\cite{huggingface_api} and the associated Python wrapper, \textit{huggingface\_hub}~\cite{huggingface_hub}, were used.
Hugging Face was queried to obtain a list of all hosted AI models, along with their download and like counts.
To minimize the risk of selecting inappropriate or personal AI models, inclusion criteria were set, requiring models to have at least 100 downloads and at least 100 likes on Hugging Face.
These criteria are commonly applied to reduce biases in software repository mining \cite{kalliamvakou2014promises}.
In total, 1,643 AI models were retrieved, representing the study population.
Due to the large size of this population, a statistically significant random sample was built with a confidence level of 95\% and a margin of error of 5\%. The sample size calculated was 312 models.
\subsection{Data Analysis}
An \aibom consists of several data fields, and when ALOHA generates an \aibom, it may not provide information for each data field.
This would be the case when the model card of the AI model (which is hosted on Hugging Face) does not report information mapped to a data field. To address the research question, ALOHA was used to generate the \aibom for each AI model in the sample, followed by a manual analysis of the missing data fields in the generated \aiboms.  
This process involved inspecting the content of the model cards on Hugging Face to understand why certain data fields are missing. 
The missing information might have been reported in another section of the model card or entirely omitted.
For each missing data field, a 'code' was assigned to explain the reason for its absence.

\section{Discussion}
The analysis of data related to the completion of fields in models uploaded to Hugging Face has revealed significant trends. Specifically, the analysis highlights the following findings:

\begin{mdframed}
    \textbf{Finding \#1}: The most frequently present fields are those that can either be manually added or automatically inferred by Hugging Face when a model is uploaded.
\end{mdframed}
These include: \textit{authors}, \textit{tags}, \textit{task}, \textit{architectureFamily}, \textit{modelArchitecture}, and \textit{library\_name}. Conversely, fields that require manual entry and cannot be automatically inferred by Hugging Face, such as \textit{description}, \textit{datasets}, \textit{performanceMetrics}, \textit{useCases}, and \textit{environmentalConsiderations}, are less frequently present. Fields like \textit{environmentalConsiderations} are rarer because they require specific data, such as CO2 emissions (in grams), which are not readily available or easily extracted.

By analyzing the data on error classification, it can be deduced that:
\begin{mdframed}
\textbf{Finding \#2}: The empty fields within the generated \aibom are primarily due to the absence of information in the model cards, as indicated by Code 01, which accounts for 77\% of the cases. 
\end{mdframed}
In a smaller portion of cases, the information is present in the model card but does not conform to the template suggested by Hugging Face. In particular, for the \textit{description} field, the main reason it is not present in the generated \aibom is that, although the information is provided, it is entered in a format that does not adhere to the Hugging Face template (68.54\%), preventing it from being extracted by the tool for \aibom generation.
\begin{mdframed}
\textbf{Finding \#3}: Many fields are missing because the information appears in an unexpected section of the model card, which doesn't comply with the Hugging Face template. This issue, linked to Code 02, accounts for 21\% of cases. 
\end{mdframed}
The \textit{description} and \textit{useCases} fields, which are extracted from the text description part of the model card, are missing due to unavailable information. This happens in a limited number of cases: 5.62\% for \textit{description} and 0.85\% for \textit{useCases}.
\begin{mdframed}
\textbf{Finding \#4}:  In rare cases, fields extracted from the text description section of the model card may be unavailable due to Code 03. This occurs because access to the model card and related information is restricted.
\end{mdframed}

These issues highlight the challenges of ensuring transparency in AI systems, particularly when critical information is not publicly accessible. Pepe~\etal~\cite{How_do_Hugging} also emphasized that restricted or incomplete documentation limits the ability to assess model reliability and compliance, which aligns with the challenges observed in this study.

\section{Threats To Validity}
In this section, the threats that might affect the results are discussed. Threats to internal validity are not discussed, as causality is not investigated.
\paragraph{Construct Validity} 
Threats to construct validity concern the relation between theory and observation ~\cite{wohlin2012experimentation}. The fields selected for the empirical evaluation represent the key fields that an \aibom should include to ensure transparency and completeness. These fields were retrieved using Hugging Face API Endpoints. 
However, the availability and correctness of these fields depend on the model authors, who are responsible for providing the information and ensuring it aligns with the expected template.
%However, the presence and content of these fields depend on the model authors, meaning whether they provided the information and whether it is accurate and compliant with the expected template.
\paragraph{Conclusion Validity}
Threats to conclusion validity concern statistical significance, including sample composition and size ~\cite{wohlin2012experimentation}. A statistically significant random sample of AI models with at least 100 downloads and at least 100 likes on Hugging Face was built, using a 95\% confidence level and a 5\% margin of error, instead of analyzing all available models. Although this is a common procedure to avoid analyzing the entire population of interest, it might pose a threat to conclusion validity, as the selected sample may not fully represent all AI models available on the platform.
\paragraph{External Validity} 
Threats to external validity concern the generalizability of results ~\cite{wohlin2012experimentation}. The results might not be generalizable to the creation of AIBOMs for models hosted on platforms other than Hugging Face, as these platforms may use different templates for model documentation. Finally, the results might not fully reflect the current state of model documentation, as the study was conducted in February 2025. Since model cards can be updated over time, some of the missing or incorrect fields observed may have been added or modified after data collection.

\chapter{Conclusion}
This research introduced ALOHA, a tool for generating \aiboms to improve transparency, security, and accountability in AI systems. 
By leveraging model cards from Hugging Face and structuring the extracted information according to the CycloneDX standard, ALOHA provides a systematic and automated approach to documenting AI models and their components.

The empirical evaluation demonstrated that while ALOHA effectively generates \aiboms, a significant portion of missing data stems from incomplete or inconsistent model documentation. This finding highlights the need for improved documentation practices within the AI community. 

A promising direction for future work is extending the tool’s compatibility to other AI model repositories beyond Hugging Face, such as TensorFlow Hub, PyTorch Hub, or private repositories. This would involve adapting the tool to handle diverse documentation formats and standards, ensuring its applicability across a broader range of platforms and ecosystems.\\
Additionally, while the tool currently generates \aiboms based on the CycloneDX standard, future research could explore extending support to other standards, such as SPDX. 
By supporting multiple standards, the tool could offer greater flexibility and interoperability, catering to a wider range of user needs and regulatory requirements.

Another valuable enhancement would be the development of a graphical user interface (GUI) for ALOHA.  
This would improve accessibility for a broader audience, including users without technical expertise, while streamlining the workflow for experienced practitioners.

The development of ALHOA provides valuable insights and tools for both practitioners and researchers in the field of AI model documentation and \aibom generation. 
For practitioners, ALOHA automates the creation of \aiboms for AI models hosted on Hugging Face, simplifying the documentation process, ensuring compliance with emerging regulatory requirements, and improving transparency in AI development. 
Additionally, ALHOA highlights the importance of well-structured and complete model documentation. Practitioners are encouraged to follow the Hugging Face model card template, making sure that all relevant fields such as model architecture, training data, intended use, and limitations are thoroughly and accurately filled out. This not only facilitates the generation of \aiboms but also enhances the reproducibility and usability of AI models. Another key aspect is integrating ALHOA into existing AI development workflows. By doing so, practitioners can automate the generation of \aibom as part of the model release process, ensuring that documentation is consistently produced and kept up-to-date.
For researchers, ALOHA provides a solid foundation for future innovations in \aiboms and AI model documentation. 
Building on this foundation, researchers could develop advanced tools for automated \aibom validation and integration with additional information sources. 
These efforts would improve the tool’s functionality and drive the adoption of standardized documentation practices across the AI community.
